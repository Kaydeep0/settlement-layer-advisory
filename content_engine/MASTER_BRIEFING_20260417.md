U.S. fiscal deficits have now surpassed private sector lending as the primary driver of economic activity.

This is not a cyclical blip. It is a structural regime change.

When the government becomes the dominant source of economic impulse, the transmission mechanism for monetary policy breaks. Rate hikes slow private borrowing but cannot offset deficit spending at scale. Inflation becomes harder to kill because its fuel source sits outside the Fed's reach.

For fixed income and private credit markets, this reshapes the risk calculus entirely. Spread compression, duration positioning, and default modeling all assume a world where monetary policy sets the tone. That assumption is increasingly outdated.

The systems-level implication: capital markets are now priced against a fiscal anchor, not a monetary one. That changes how you think about everything from sovereign spreads to private credit underwriting.

Are credit markets actually repricing for fiscal dominance, or are we still running old models on new terrain?

**Template:** B — Deep Observation

**Hashtags:** #CapitalMarkets #Macro #PrivateCredit  
**Characters:** 996

Most people think banks lend out deposits. They don't. Banks create deposits when they lend. The liability and the asset are born simultaneously.

This matters more than most investors realize. When credit expands, broad money grows. When loans are repaid or written off, money contracts. The Fed doesn't fully control this. Commercial banks do, through their collective lending decisions.

What gets missed: monetary inflation isn't just a central bank story. It's a banking system story. If credit demand collapses or lending standards tighten hard, you can have loose Fed policy and still get disinflation. Japan ran that experiment for decades.

So when you're building a macro view, the question isn't only "what is the Fed doing?" It's "what are banks doing with their balance sheets right now?"

Are we in a credit expansion or a quiet contraction?

Source: Lyn Alden, "Fractional Reserve Banking 101" (lynalden.com/fractional-reserve-banking)
